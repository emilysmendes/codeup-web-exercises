10 fun facts about our National Parks:


YellowStone
Yellowstone National Park (in Wyoming, Idaho, and Montana) is the oldest U.S. national park, founded in 1872.

Yosemite
Three of the 10 highest waterfalls in the world are located in Yosemite National Park in California.

Grand Canyon
The Grand Canyon, in Grand Canyon National Park in Arizona, is known as one of the Seven Natural Wonders of the World.

Sequoia National Park
When a giant sequoia tree fell and blocked a road in Sequoia National Park in California in 1937, the National Park Service simply created a tunnel through the 275-foot by 21-foot tree.

Hot Springs National Park
The smallest national park is Hot Springs National Park in Arkansas, coming in at about 5,500 acres. This was actually the first federally protected piece of land in 1832. It was not named a national park until 1921.

Rocky Mountain
Trail Ridge Road in Rocky Mountain National Park, which connects Grand Lake and Estes Park, is the highest continuously paved road in the U.S., and it includes breathtaking views and vistas along its entire 48 miles.

Hawaiian Volcanoes National Park
Mauna Loa Volcano in Hawaii Volcanoes National Park is the largest volcano on earth both in terms of volume and height above its base. It contains about 19,000 cubic miles of lava and rises more than 50,000 feet above its base, including the portion which is beneath the ocean.

Isle Royale
Isle Royale National Park is the only major national park to close entirely for the winter because of the difficulty of travel and hazards of wilderness survival during Michigan's winters.

Olympic National Park
Bears have not killed a single person in Olympic National Park, but a mountain goat, which is not a native species to the region, killed a man in 2010.

Crater Lake
At 1,932 feet deep, Crater Lake National Park in Oregon is the deepest lake in the U.S.

Parks List

Yosemite National Park

  • Yosemite is home to one of the tallest waterfalls in the world.
  • The park’s diverse landscape supports more than 400 species.
  • Yosemite might be our nation’s 3rd national park, but it sparked the idea of national parks.
  • Yosemite’s granite rock formations glow like fire at sunset.
  • Yosemite is one of the few places in the U.S. where you can see a rainbow at night, otherwise known as lunar rainbows or moonbows.

Grand Canyon

  • Grand Canyon National Park is bigger than the entire state of Rhode Island.
  • The canyon is home to a rare species of pink rattlesnakes.
  • The air at the Grand Canyon is among the cleanest air in the United States.
  • The Grand Canyon is so big and so diverse that visitors can actually experience completely different ecosystems.
  • The Hopi Tribe considers the Grand Canyon a gateway to the afterlife.

Redwood National Forest

  • The oldest living Redwood is said to be nearly 2,500 years old. It would have been a seedling during the time of the Roman Empire.
  • There are around 400 very rare Ghost Redwood trees scattered about our North Coast region. These trees, stripped of all color, remove poisons from the soil and receive needed sugars from healthier Coast Redwoods in order to survive.
  • Over 1.5 million people visit Redwood National and State Parks annually.
  • Only 10% of the initial 2 million acres of redwoods are still standing today
  • The Hyperion tree is the tallest tree on earth according to scientists. At 380 feet (116m) the tree surpassed the Stratosphere Giant in 2006 to become the tallest tree.
